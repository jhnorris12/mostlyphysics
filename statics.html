Free Body Diagrams & Equilibrium

Statics

In statics, every object is in equilibrium — the sum of all forces and moments is zero. That gives you three equations (ΣFx = 0, ΣFy = 0, ΣM = 0) to solve for unknown support reactions or internal forces. The free body diagram is where every problem begins: isolate the body and draw every external force acting on it.

Truss Analysis

Statics

How do you find the force in every member of a truss? There are two methods. The method of joints works from one joint at a time — every joint must be in equilibrium. The method of sections lets you cut through the truss and analyze a portion of it directly, which is faster when you only need specific members. Both assume members carry only axial (tension or compression) forces.

Method of Joints

Apply equilibrium at each joint to find member forces. Worked examples from start to finish, including how to identify zero-force members.

Frames & Machines

Statics

Unlike trusses, frames and machines contain members that carry more than just axial loads — they can have bending and shear. The analysis strategy is to disassemble the structure at its internal pins, draw a free body diagram for each piece, and apply equilibrium to each piece separately. Internal pin forces appear in equal and opposite pairs on adjacent members.

Distributed Loads

Statics

How do you handle a load that is spread over a length rather than applied at a single point? Replace the distributed load with a single resultant force equal to the area of the load diagram, acting at the centroid of that area. For uniform loads it's simple; for triangular or trapezoidal distributions, you need to locate the centroid carefully.
