Common questions

Students ask us these
all the time.

How do you draw a free body diagram?

Isolate the object, then draw every force acting on it — weight (mg downward), normal force (perpendicular to surface), tension along the rope, friction opposing motion. Label each with a variable. This is the starting point for every Newton's law problem. See our Forces & Newton's Laws video for worked examples.

Which kinematic equation should I use?

List what you know and what you're solving for. The four equations each leave out one variable: no time, no final velocity, no displacement, or no acceleration. Match what's missing to the equation that doesn't use it. Our Kinematics video walks through this decision process with multiple problems.

What is the difference between electric potential and electric potential energy?

Electric potential (V) is a property of a point in space — it tells you the potential energy per unit charge at that location. Electric potential energy (U) is the energy stored by a specific charge at that point: U = qV. The distinction matters when you're working with multiple charges or moving charges through a field.

How do you find the reactions in a truss?

Start with the whole truss as a rigid body: apply ΣFx = 0, ΣFy = 0, and ΣM = 0 to find the support reactions. Then use the method of joints or method of sections to find forces in individual members. Every member is assumed two-force (axial only). See our Statics playlist.

What is the best way to study for a Physics I final exam?

Work problems — not just read notes. For each major topic (kinematics, Newton's laws, energy, momentum, rotation), solve at least 3–5 problems from scratch without looking at solutions first. Our Physics I Final Exam Review video covers every topic with worked examples in one sitting.

How do you apply Kirchhoff's laws to circuits?

KVL (voltage law): the sum of voltage drops around any closed loop equals zero. KCL (current law): the sum of currents entering any node equals the sum leaving. Label unknown currents, assign loop directions, write one equation per unknown, then solve the system. Physics II circuit videos cover this with multi-loop examples.
